I listened to episode 2 of this new york times audio series hosted by Nikole Hannah-Jones. I am sharing 3 takeaways and thoughts here. 

(1) The Rise and Fall of Global Market Conditions: punishments to slaves rose and fell with the price of cotton. When the crop prices rose in the English world (e.g. Manchester), workers across the world (in Alabama, Georgia) felt it with a whip. 
⚠️What does this mean today? There is no check to prevent human harm in reaction to market fluctuations today. The first people to feel the effects of market fluctuations will not be CEOs and white leaders of fortune 500 companies, but instead will be BIPOC employees (who feel the effect through lay offs, pay cuts, or worse). They have systematically been forced into vulnerable roles (that our laws do little to protect) by lack of generational opportunity and wealth that was stolen by white people. We are still enabling this.  

(2) American Capitalism's Obsession with Quotas: capitalistic technique of using quotas to drive productivity have roots in the cotton farming slave industry. The modern-day process of worrying about punitive action if you're beneath your quota, or getting it bumped if you're above, is a standard that was developed from expectations put on slaves to maximize cotton output, when punitive action was delivered in blood and not through salary. This is a twisted artifact in the way our modern corporations are run. 
⚠️ What does this mean today? It gives a chilling unspoken undertone to of white management setting quotas for BIPOC, which I hadn't thought of before. The fact that this piece of history wasn't covered in economy lessons and is accepted around me as normal practice speaks to the blind eye we've turned to the past. This is not okay.

(3) Markets enabling anonymization: White people bought slaves with mortgages. White people would use human beings they owned as mortgage collateral to buy more human beings. When plantations were profitable, and payments were made on these mortgages, investors would get a cut. This allowed for regions (e.g. Britain) where slavery had been eradicated generations ago to bankroll and profit from it still.  
⚠️ What does this mean today? We could be darkly investing in modern-day slavery, and be completely unaware of it. There is no strong way to prevent a large electronics company from taking advantage of people in other areas of the world unprotected even by the US' slim set of worker's rights. Personally, after listening to this discussion, I can't play unaware anymore (which of course, in itself is problematic - I need to be responsible for my own education as I navigate the world) and continue to let my money sit in a generic mix of ETFs that could be funding modern day slavery. When markets open on Monday I am pulling from these ETF investments and re-investing in B corps (likely KRMA) while I take time to educate myself on where I can fund good- perhaps something like this Portfolia fund. 


Additional takeaways:
- (podcast title) The Economy That Slaves Built: Height of slavery, combined value of all enslved people was more than all railroads/factories combined. This. is. what. built. the. foundation. of. America's. economy. and. wealth. 

- New laws, new workarounds: When slavery was outlawed, slaves were sold South where it was still legal. What does this mean today? When new legislation is introduced, there needs to be a clear plan that people profiteering off don't find loopholes to further harm people it's meant to protect. Think: when we enforce fair pay and working conditions in the US, we must ensure our companies have to hold themselves to the same standards in other countries they operate in (and no shadow/contracting companies).
